Week 0: Basecamp and Orientation

29 August 2023

Well, we're off to the races! Here is the first post of this blog. It's a somewhat daunting task to get started, but I'll do my best.

Last week, I attended my cohort's Basecamp and first Access Weekend. The "Basecamp" component involved an orientation to the program as a whole. It featured discussions concerning class times, logistics, and the cornucopia of resources available to my cohort during and after our time in the program. (Notably, a variety of team-building activities, including a high-ropes course and an impromptu volleyball game, were involved.) Meanwhile, the "Access Weekend" component involved attending the first actual classes of the program. The events lasted from Thursday through Sunday afternoon, and it was a blur of activity, new people, and free food. Although I had attended a few in-person admitted students events earlier in the year -- such as a summer Pirates game -- this weekend was the first time my entire cohort had gotten together in person.

My cohort (PT26, short for "Part-Time 2026") is cool. I can't remember the exact number, but I'm pretty sure Meg (the Director of the part-time program) said that there are roughly 120 students in my group. Notably, that's 120 people enrolled in a rigorous graduate program while managing full-time jobs, families, hobbies, and all of the other things that life entails.

If the demographics of the PT25 cohort are any indication of my own cohort's demographics, I fall squarely in the middle: I just turned 30, I'm a white male, I live in the Mid-Atlantic, and I have a few years of work experience under my belt. In my cohort, there are product managers and recent graduates. There are fellow staff members of CMU, as well as at least one doctor and one surgeon. There are members of the Armed Forces. One younger-looking student has been to all 50 states and north of 50 countries, and another individual lives in Paris. Already there are folks wanting to join intramural teams and clubs (myself included). It's a solid group.

Perhaps the one area where I stand out the most is my undergraduate degree. While the demographics of the program illustrate that nearly 90% of part-timers majored in business, finance, or a STEM-related field, I proudly studied Anthropology and Music at a small liberal arts school. In undergrad, I chose Anthropology because it was interesting to me and because I knew that I would always be working with people. Meanwhile, I minored in Music because I knew that it will always be a hobby of mine. That being said, I had a moment of profound joy when one of the first slides of the first lecture of the program had "Defining Culture" in big bold letters at the top. I knew I had a home at a quant-oriented program, but it sure was nice to see it in writing.

Throughout the three-year program, my classmates and I will each be taking two courses at a time, typically once a week in the evening. Each class is called a "Mini", and they last about seven weeks. Like everyone else in my cohort, my first two minis are called "Managing People and Teams" and "Probability and Statistics". I am already grateful for the balance between one quantitative course and one qualitative course. As someone who didn't take any math classes in college, a condition of my admission into the program involved passing an online "Math for Management" course before the Basecamp weekend. I needed to get an 80% or above, and I had two attempts. I got an impressively low 49% on the first attempt and then, after going through the modules and spending five hours on the test itself, scored a 91%. I quickly befriended a number of other students who also struggled through the "Math for Management" class, and we relived our experience together. I've honestly enjoyed math because I like the puzzle-solving aspect of it all, but that test sure stressed me out. I'm glad I wasn't the only one.

One last thing I'll mention for now is the student panel. Over lunch during one day of the Basecamp component, there was a lunch-and-learn featuring a panel of five current part-time MBA students. The panel included a father of three looking to change industries; someone who had recently been hired after 18 months of looking for a job; and someone with a fantastically impressive-sounding title at a major tech company. When I asked the panel what they hoped to gain by going through the MBA program, I was struck by how each of them stated their desire to learn and grow their respective careers, regardless of where they worked now or how much money they made. I've always taken the mindset that I'll never really know what I want to be when I grow up, but I'll do my best at wherever I go, and I'll through perseverance and a healthy sense of humor. Ironically, the panelist with the fancy tech job title said the same thing.

I'm looking forward to the adventures to come.
